The individual adoption stages in Part 3 are adapted from Steve Yegge's "Evolution of the Programmer" model and Daniel Jones's "11 Stages of Agentic Coding."

The Theory-of-Constraints framing in Part 3 applies Eliyahu Goldratt's principle that optimising any step other than the binding constraint reduces throughput.

The adoption sequence in Part 4 draws on Bryan Finster's work on the sequencing of delivery improvements.

The specification-driven development position in Part 5 builds on foundational work by Dan North (Introducing BDD), Gojko Adzic (Specification by Example), John Smart (BDD in Action), and Matt Wynne (Example Mapping). The framework's position is that the current industry interest in spec-driven AI development is a rediscovery of these practices, made newly practical by agents that need unambiguous specifications. The three-layer specification hierarchy and spec evolution practices are this framework's contribution; the underlying discipline is theirs. The emerging SDD tooling landscape draws on practice documented by GitHub (Spec Kit), Thoughtworks, AWS (Kiro), and Brix Consulting (nWave), reframed within an XP and Continuous Discovery context.

The spec evolution practices in Part 5 draw on analysis by Lilia Abdulina and Vitaly Sharovatov (BeyondQuality) regarding the severed feedback loop in AI-accelerated development, and on Bryan Finster's persistent task memory mechanisms in his agentic-dev-team implementation. Steve Yegge's MEOW stack and Beads in Gas Town, and re:cinq's temporal knowledge graph in Lore, provided independent triangulation that a durable state outside the agent is a structural necessity.

The automated extraction analysis in Part 5 draws on re:cinq's Lore platform as a concrete example of mechanised spec evolution, and examines its limitations regarding authority modelling, embedding-based invalidation, and tacit knowledge capture.

The mutation-testing framing in the "verifying the verifier" section of Part 5 draws on the extensive academic literature beginning with DeMillo, Lipton, and Sayward (1978) and operationalised in contemporary tools; its application as a routine quality gate for AI-authored tests is a specific adaptation for the agentic workflow context.

The eval-driven development position in Part 5 draws on the TDAD framework (Test-Driven AI Development) and DeepEval as early but directionally sound work on quality practices for non-deterministic systems.

The quality coach role in Part 6 applies Matthew Skelton and Manuel Pais's Team Topologies enabling team concept to the transformation of the QA function under agentic workflows.

The team design questions in Part 8 draw on Matthew Skelton and Manuel Pais's Team Topologies framework. The cognitive load measurement questions reference work by Skelton, Pais, Weis, and Morgadas.

The management philosophy discussion in Part 7 draws on Douglas McGregor's Theory X and Theory Y.

The governance gap analysis draws on Stuart Winter-Tear's work on AI governance proof standards, specifically the concepts of conflict arbitration, total cost of ownership, and the board-level "underwriting pack."

The adoption principles in Part 8 are derived from the reasoning patterns in the Future Friendly manifesto by Brad Frost, Luke Wroblewski, Scott Jenson, and others, reframed for technology adoption contexts.

Jason Gorman's work on the relationship between engineering discipline and AI-assisted development provided independent validation of the framework's core thesis that engineering foundations become more important under AI acceleration, not less.
